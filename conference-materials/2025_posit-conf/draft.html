Enter: Behavioral Science

What is it? The interdisciplinary study of how people make decisions
(Spoiler: irrationally, yet predictably & consistently)

Classic Example: The Paradox of Choice

Researchers: Sheena Iyengar & Mark Lepper

Setting: Upscale grocery store sampling booth

Design: 24 jams vs. 6 jams display

10x higher conversion with fewer options

  • [75 seconds]
  • Behavioral science studies how humans think, feel and act‚Ä¶often in the context of decision making
  • The field itself is a blend of psychology, economics, neuroscience, and sociology‚Ä¶and a common theme is that people are often irrational‚Ä¶but in surprisingly consistent ways
  • For us as developers, this is actually great news. It means user behavior is predictable and we can design for it
  • [CLICK] Let me share the famous jam study that mirrors our dashboard problem perfectly
  • Researchers set up a fancy grocery store display with either 24 or 6 jam varieties
  • The big display attracted browsers‚Ä¶60% stopped to look versus 40% for the small display
  • [CLICK] But here‚Äôs the kicker‚Ä¶only 3% bought jam after seeing 24 options, while 30% bought after seeing just 6
  • [CLICK] That‚Äôs a 10x conversion difference!
  • More choice attracted attention but paralyzed action
  • Sound familiar? That‚Äôs exactly what our 18-filter dashboard was doing
  • And here‚Äôs what this means for your development workflow: every filter you add isn‚Äôt just a feature‚Ä¶it‚Äôs a cognitive tax on your users
  • When we understand these psychological principles, we can predict which features will actually get used versus which ones will just add to your maintenance burden
  • This isn‚Äôt soft science‚Ä¶it‚Äôs engineering intelligence that saves you from building things nobody will use

Applied to Dashboards

Core Concepts

  • Cognitive Load
    • Too many choices overwhelm users‚Äô mental capacity
  • Progressive Disclosure
    • Reveal complexity gradually as users need it
  • Framing Effects
    • Same data can tell completely different stories
  • Choice Architecture
    • Guide decisions without restricting options

Dashboard Examples

  • Cognitive Load
    • Start with 3 key filters, hide 15 advanced ones
  • Progressive Disclosure
    • Show summary ‚Üí detailed dive ‚Üí comparisons
  • Framing Effects
    • ‚ÄúRevenue up 15%‚Äù vs ‚ÄúRevenue missed by 5%‚Äù
  • Choice Architecture
    • Smart defaults, logical grouping, visual hierarchy
  • [55 seconds]
  • Now the paradox of choice is just one of many behavioral science concepts that directly apply to dashboards
  • But the cool thing is that these aren‚Äôt abstract theories‚Ä¶they‚Äôre engineering principles
  • Cognitive Load‚Ä¶Your users‚Äô brains have RAM limits. Every filter costs processing power. And unlike computer RAM, you can‚Äôt just add more
  • Progressive Disclosure‚Ä¶Nobody reads the manual. Show the basics, then reveal complexity as needed. Think of it like lazy loading for human cognition
  • Framing Effects‚Ä¶‚ÄúRevenue up 15%‚Äù versus ‚ÄúMissed target by 5%‚Äù‚Ä¶same data, vastly different user reactions. Your PM sees success, your CFO sees failure
  • Choice Architecture‚Ä¶You‚Äôre not limiting options, you‚Äôre creating a path through them. Like good API design‚Ä¶guide the happy path
  • [CLICK] In practice, this means starting with 3 filters not 18, showing summaries before details, and using smart defaults
  • For Shiny developers specifically, this means using updateSelectInput() to dynamically adjust options based on user behavior, not showing everything upfront
  • It means conditional panels that appear when needed, not cluttering the initial view
  • And it means your reactive expressions should anticipate common user paths, pre-computing likely next steps
  • This is engineering the experience as carefully as the backend
  • [end by 5:15]
